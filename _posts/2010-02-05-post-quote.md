## Response 1: On the Importance of Context for Visual Communication

This response reflects on how visualizations communicate meaning and how an LLM interprets them when given minimal context. To test this, I provided an LLM with a screenshot of my visualizations and asked: *“What do you understand from looking at these visuals only with no context?”* The goal was to evaluate whether the visuals could stand on their own and how meaning is constructed without additional explanation.


![LLM Interpretation of Visualizations]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/LLM.png)

*Figure 1: LLM Interpretation of Visualizations*


The LLM’s response was, at first glance, highly accurate. It correctly identified that the visuals were measuring “psychological interiority” by tracking how often words like *felt* and *thought* appear near the names Draco and Severus. It also successfully distinguished between canonical texts and fanfiction, noting that fanfiction shows higher levels of interiority for both characters. Additionally, it described this measurement as a “proxy” for how much access readers are given to a character’s inner life, which aligns closely with the intended design of the analysis.

At this point, it may seem that the visuals are self-explanatory and require little additional context. However, a closer look suggests that the LLM’s interpretation depends heavily on information already embedded within the visuals. Elements such as the chart title, axis labels, and terminology—especially phrases like “interiority” and “normalized by mentions”—provide strong interpretive cues. Rather than interpreting raw data alone, the model is guided by these design choices, which shape how the visualization is read.

This challenges the idea that visuals “speak for themselves.” Instead, it suggests that effective visual communication works by embedding context directly into the visualization. The LLM did not independently derive the concept of interiority or the reasoning behind using “felt” and “thought.” Instead, it relied on the framing already present in the chart. Even its use of the word “proxy” reflects an assumption about how language relates to psychological depth—an assumption built into the methodology rather than discovered from the data.

This aligns with Lisnic et al.’s argument that misleading visualizations are rarely caused by poor design alone, but rather by how viewers interpret and frame the data. As they show, most misleading charts are not visually deceptive but become misleading through reasoning errors such as overgeneralization or unsupported claims.

At the same time, the LLM’s response includes subtle overinterpretations. For instance, it claims that Draco has “zero (or near-zero)” interiority in the canon, which simplifies variation across texts. It also suggests that fanfiction writers are more interested in characters’ internal lives, moving beyond description into broader interpretation. These claims are not entirely incorrect, but they demonstrate how easily data-driven observations can turn into generalized conclusions.

This reflects a key concern raised by Ted Underwood in *The Risks of Distant Reading*, where he argues that computational methods reveal patterns but cannot fully explain them without interpretation. Visualizations compress complex textual data into simplified forms, making comparison possible but also increasing the risk of overgeneralization. The LLM’s response illustrates this tension: it successfully identifies patterns, yet extends them into claims that require human judgment to evaluate.

Another important factor is visual literacy. The LLM’s ability to interpret the charts correctly suggests that it recognizes common visualization conventions, such as bar charts indicating comparison and line graphs indicating trends. However, not all viewers would necessarily interpret the visuals in the same way. Without an understanding of normalization or proximity-based word analysis, a reader might misunderstand what the data represents.

Ultimately, this experiment demonstrates that context is not separate from visualization but can be built into it. The clarity of the visuals depends on how effectively they guide interpretation through labels, structure, and terminology. At the same time, the LLM’s response shows that even well-designed visuals can lead to confident but slightly overstated conclusions when taken on their own.

This suggests that strong visual communication requires a balance. Visuals should present patterns clearly, but they must also be supported by written explanation that clarifies their meaning, limitations, and assumptions. Rather than functioning independently, visuals work best as part of a broader interpretive framework.
